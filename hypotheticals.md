## Hypothetical questions

### Sources
 - https://riftbound.leagueoflegends.com/en-us/news/announcements/march-2026-tournament-rules-update--changelog/
 - TR Rule 204.5
 - Judge discussion with UVS

### Reasonning

The objectives of theses rules are : 
 - Judges make sure rules are followed in game.
 - Judges do not provide strategic advantage.
 - Judges answer rules questions as much a possible.
 - Judges provide the best customer service for all players as possible.

Judges should not answer question about the board state for an action that has not been taken yet. (TR204.5.a.1).
Judges can still answer hypothetical questions about cards, cards interactions and rules.

As judges we should be guiding the players into asking a question we are able to answer. It's not about *not answering* the question, or playing word games.  
We should actually encourage the players to ask ruling questions without refering to the game state.

There are several ways to do it : 
 - Ask : "What rule interaction are your asking about ?"
 - Give an example of acceptable question : "The question asked is not one I am currently able to answer. However, you can rephrase your question to be specific to the rules and I will be able to answer, such as *asking if a point scoring ability is able to score the final point.*"

If language gets in the way of the discussion, ask the player the languages they speak and try to find a translator.  
At any time, if a judge is not sure how to answer an hypothetical question, they can ask another judge or escalate to a HJ.

Side notes : 
- Player are allowed to ask questions to the judge away from the table.
- Players can ask about cards and interactions that are not in the current game state. Judges can provides Watcher references, in those cases too.

### Example 1 : 
A player has a unit on the battlefield Vilemaw's lair (Units can't move from here to base.)  
That player asks : *"Can I play Flash (Move up to 2 friendly units to base) on my unit at this battlefield ?"*

We can't answer that question directly, because it's about the current state of the game.  
The player reformulates : *"Can I play Flash on a friendly unit at Vilemaw's lair ?"*

This is now not a question about the game state. The answer could be *yes* but as a judge we know that interaction is more complex. (They can ask about target legality for flash, or ask if the unit will actually go back to base)  
We don't want to give any extra information so a *"What specific ruling are your asking about ?"* seems the more appropriate answer to find that out without giving extra information.

### Example 2 : 
A player has a 3 might unit on a battlefield, is playing against a red deck and asks :  
*"Would hextech ray kill this unit?"*

Instead of simply saying yes or no you could say :  
*"Hextech ray would deal 3 damage to the unit, which would kill it if it has 3 or less might"*.
